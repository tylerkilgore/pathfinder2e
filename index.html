1. The Core Mechanic: 3 Actions & The 4 Degrees of Success

Your Turn is 3 Actions

In PF2e, you get 3 Actions on your turn. You also get 1 Reaction.

  • You can use your 3 actions for anything.
  • Want to attack three times? You can (with a penalty, see below).
  • Want to move, cast a spell, and attack? You can.
  • Want to move three times? You can.

Common Actions:

  • Strike (1 Action): Make a single melee or ranged attack.
  • Stride (1 Action): Move up to your Speed.
  • Step (1 Action): Move 5 feet. This does not trigger Attacks of Opportunity.
  • Cast a Spell (1-3 Actions): Most spells cost 2 Actions, but some are 1, 3, or even a Reaction. This is listed in the spell's stat block.
  • Raise a Shield (1 Action): Gain your shield's AC bonus until the start of your next turn.
  • Skill Actions: Many skills have associated actions, like Demoralize (1 Action) to frighten a foe, Treat Wounds (10 min) to heal, or Hide (1 Action) to become hidden.

The "Multiple Attack Penalty" (MAP)

You can attack three times, but it's usually not a good idea.

  • Your 1st Strike on your turn is made at your full bonus.
  • Your 2nd Strike on your turn takes a -5 penalty (-4 for agile weapons).
  • Your 3rd (and subsequent) Strike takes a -10 penalty (-8 for agile weapons).

This penalty applies to any attack roll, including Strikes and some spells.

The Four Degrees of Success

In PF2e, there are four possible outcomes for almost any roll (attacks, saves, skill checks):

  • Critical Success: You beat the DC by 10 or more.
  • Success: You beat the DC (but not by 10 or more).
  • Failure: You fail to meet the DC (but not by 10 or more).
  • Critical Failure: You fail the DC by 10 or more.

Example: Attack Roll

  • Crit Success: Double damage.
  • Success: Normal damage.
  • Failure: You miss.
  • Crit Failure: You miss, and might have a penalty (like dropping your weapon, depending on the ability).

Spells, in particular, often have effects for all four degrees. A successful save against a Fireball, for instance, might mean you take half damage, while a critical success means you take no damage.

2. Character Creation: The "ABCs"

Character creation in PF2e is Ancestry, Background, Class.

  • Ancestry (replaces Race): This is your heritage (Human, Elf, Dwarf, Goblin, etc.). It gives you a "heritage" (like a subrace), a starting Ancestry Feat, ability boosts/flaws, and HP. You get new Ancestry Feats at levels 5, 9, 13, and 17.
  • Background (replaces 5e Background): This gives you two ability boosts, a Skill Feat, and proficiency in two skills.
  • Class (replaces 5e Class): This is your job (Fighter, Wizard, etc.). It gives you ability boosts, HP, and a ton of Class Feats.

Feats, Feats, and More Feats!

You get a different feat at almost every level.

  • Class Feats: These define your build. A Fighter might choose feats that improve their shield use, while another Fighter picks feats for two-handed power attacks. This is how you "specialize" your class.
  • Skill Feats: These make you better at skills in non-combat ways (e.g., "Intimidating Glare" lets you Demoralize without speaking).
  • General Feats: A grab-bag of useful abilities (e.g., "Incredible Initiative" or "Toughness").
  • Ancestry Feats: These connect you to your heritage (e.g., "Elven Weapon Familiarity").

3. Combat & Tactics

  • Attacks of Opportunity (AoO): Most characters do NOT have this. Only specific classes (like the Fighter) and some monsters get AoO as a special reaction.
  • Conditions: Conditions are very important and granular. Instead of just "Poisoned," you might have a condition with a value, like Frightened 1 (a -1 to all checks and DCs) or Persistent Damage 1d6 (you take 1d6 damage at the end of your turn). These values often decrease by 1 each round.
  • Dying: When you hit 0 HP, you are Dying. You make a "Recovery Check" (a flat check, like a 5e death save) each round.
    • Dying 1: You're down.
    • Dying 2: You're in trouble.
    • Dying 3: You're really in trouble.
    • Dying 4: You are dead.
  • Wounded: When you get healed from 0 HP, you gain the Wounded 1 condition. If you go down again while Wounded, your Dying value starts at your Wounded value.

4. Magic & Spells

  • Spell Levels: PF2e has 10 spell levels, not 9. Level 10 spells are epic, world-changing magic.
  • Cantrips: Cantrips are still 0-level spells, but they automatically heighten (scale) as you level up. You don't need to track different damage dice.
  • Spell Slots: These work very similarly to 5e for "prepared" casters like Wizards and Clerics. You prepare a specific spell in a specific slot.
  • Spontaneous Casters: Casters like Sorcerers and Bards have a "Spell Repertoire" (spells known) and can cast any of them using their slots, just like a 5e Sorcerer.
  • Focus Spells: A "per-encounter" power. You get a number of Focus Points (usually 1-3). You can spend a point to cast a spell. You can Refocus (a 10-minute activity) to get your points back.

5. The Math: "Proficiency + Level"

In PF2e, your proficiency is tied to your Level.

Your main bonus = Ability Mod + Proficiency Bonus + Level

  • Proficiency Bonus:
    • Untrained: +0
    • Trained: +2
    • Expert: +4
    • Master: +6
    • Legendary: +8

This means at Level 1, a Fighter with 18 STR (a +4 mod) and "Trained" in Longswords (+2) has an attack bonus of: +4 (Str) + 2 (Trained) + 1 (Level) = +7

At Level 20, that same Fighter with 22 STR (+6 mod) and "Legendary" in Longswords (+8) has an attack bonus of: +6 (Str) + 8 (Legendary) + 20 (Level) = +34

What this means:

  1. Numbers get big!
  2. Every +1 matters. A +1 bonus is a 5% better chance to hit and a 5% better chance to crit.
  3. You are expected to keep pace. You must fight "on-level" monsters. A monster that is 4 levels above you is a boss monster and will be incredibly dangerous.

Pathfinder 2e: The 3-Action Economy

On your turn, you get:

  • 3 Actions (use them for anything!)
  • 1 Reaction (used on anyone's turn, including your own)
  • Free Actions (don't cost any of the above)

The Golden Rule: MAP

MAP = Multiple Attack Penalty

Your second attack (Strike) on your turn gets a -5 penalty. Your third gets a -10.

  • 1st Strike: +0
  • 2nd Strike: -5 (or -4 for "agile" weapons)
  • 3rd+ Strike: -10 (or -8 for "agile" weapons)

    1. Stride up to an enemy.
    2. Strike the enemy (at +0 MAP).

    1. Stride up to an enemy.
    2. Strike the enemy (at +0 MAP).
    3. Strike again (at -5 MAP).

    1. Cast a 2-Action Heal on an ally.
    2. Stride away from an enemy.
  • Skirmishing Rogue:

    2. Step 5 feet away (doesn't trigger AoO).
    3. Hide behind a pillar.
  • Blaster Sorcerer:
    1. Cast a 2-Action Fireball.
    2. Demoralize an enemy with your last action.

How PF2e Spellcasters Prepare Spells

This guide breaks down the two main systems for "preparing" spells in Pathfinder 2nd Edition, as well as the universal "Focus Spell" system.

1. Prepared Spellcasters (The "Vancian" System)

These casters have access to a large list of spells but must meticulously plan their day.

  • Classes: Wizard, Cleric, Druid, Witch, Magus
  • How it Works: Each morning during your "Daily Preparations," you must choose a specific spell to place into each of your spell slots.
  • Example: You have three "1st-level" slots. You must decide now that the first holds Magic Missile, the second holds Grease, and the third also holds Magic Missile.
  • Casting: To cast Grease, you must expend the slot you prepared it in. You cannot use your third slot (which holds Magic Missile) to cast Grease, even though it's the same spell level.
  • Primary Pro: Incredible day-to-day versatility. You can change your entire spell list to match expected challenges.
  • Primary Con: No flexibility in the moment. If you prepared Magic Missile three times, you cannot cast Grease.

Variations on Prepared Casters:

  • Wizard: Knows all common 0-level spells. For all other levels, they can only prepare spells that are in their spellbook. They add spells to their book by leveling up or by scribing from scrolls.
  • Cleric & Druid: These casters know their entire class spell list automatically. A 5th-level Druid has access to every single 1st, 2nd, and 3rd-level spell on the Primal spell list and can prepare any of them.
  • Witch: Functions like a Wizard, but their spellbook is their Familiar. They must have their familiar nearby to prepare spells.
  • Magus: A "bounded" caster. They are prepared casters, but have far fewer spell slots that top out at a lower level.

2. Spontaneous Spellcasters (The "Repertoire" System)

These casters are all about flexibility in the moment, at the cost of day-to-day variety.

  • Classes: Sorcerer, Bard, Oracle, Psychic, Summoner
  • How it Works: These casters do not prepare spells. Instead, they have a small, fixed list of spells they know called a Spell Repertoire.
  • Example: You have three "1st-level" slots. Your repertoire is a list of 2-3 spells you know for that level.
  • Casting: You can use any of your spell slots to cast any spell in your repertoire of that level or lower. If you have three 1st-level slots and know Heal and Magic Missile, you can cast Heal three times, Magic Missile three times, or any other combination until you run out of slots.
  • Primary Pro: Maximum on-the-fly flexibility. You always have your entire (small) toolbox available.
  • Primary Con: Very little day-to-day versatility. You can only change the spells in your repertoire when you level up.
  • Signature Spells: Spontaneous casters get "Signature Spells." A signature spell is automatically heightened to any spell level you can cast. For other spells, you must learn them at a specific level.

3. The "Not-a-Caster" Caster: The Alchemist

The Alchemist is in a category all their own.

  • How it Works: Alchemists do not have spell slots or a spell repertoire.
  • Preparation: Each day, they prepare a batch of Infused Reagents.
  • "Casting": As a 1 or 2-action ability, they can spend their Infused Reagents to craft any alchemical item from their Formula Book on the-fly.

4. The Universal System: Focus Spells

This is a separate system that almost all spellcasters (and even some non-casters!) use.

  • What they are: Special spells unique to your class or subclass.
  • How they work: You have a small pool of Focus Points (usually 1-3).
  • Casting: Casting a Focus Spell costs 1 Focus Point.
  • Resetting: You get your Focus Points back by using the Refocus activity (10 minutes).
